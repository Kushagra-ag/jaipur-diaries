History

The City Palace was actually built as a residence for the royal families of Jaipur. Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh II is known to have commissioned the work for building the outer wall of the city's complex. He shifted from Amer to Jaipur due to water problems and an increase in population in 1727. Thereafter, it was built between 1727 and 1732 by Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh II, the founder of the city of Jaipur, who also built the Hawa Mahal and the Jantar Mantar.

Architecture

The royal City Palace is situated at the center of the city. One of the most admired and finest attractions among the places to visit in the Pink City, with an organized layout of large courtyards, perfectly manicured gardens, and magnificent mahals, the palace is still a home to the royal family of Jaipur.

The City Palace complex is spread over a large area occupying one seventh of the old city of Jaipur. The architecture of City Palace Jaipur, built in red and pink stones, is based on a fusion combining Rajputana, Mughal and European styles of architecture and built on the principles of, Indian Science of Architecture, Vastu. It was built under the guidance of Sawai Jai Singh II by two renowned architects- Vidyadhar Bhattacharya and Sir Samuel Swinton Jacob. Its outer boundary was built by Raja Jai Singh and other structures by his successors to add charm to it.

Museum- Part of the City Palace Jaipur is a museum, which is now called Maharaja Sawai Man Singh Museum, which houses the largest and the finest collection of arms in India. It includes swords(The sword of Maharaja Man Singh), double-edged cutters, daggers, knives, axes and arrows of various shapes and sizes.

Chandra Mahal- The other part is the residence of the descendents of the former rulers of Jaipur. The royal family resides in the Chandra Mahal which is a seven-storied cream-white structure, sumptuously adorned with unique paintings and floral decorations with mirrored walls and ceilings. It has screened balconies and a pavilion at the roof from where a panoramic view of the city can be seen. It is set amidst well laid out gardens and a decorative lake in the foreground.

Entrance Gate- Planned in a 'Grid Style', this palace is impressive with three huge gates for entry and exit to the complex, namely, Udai Pol, Tripolia Gate(three gates) and Virendra Pol. City Palace Jaipur can be accessed through any entrance gate except for Tripolia Gate which is reserved for the royal family.

Pritam Niwas Chowk- The Pritam Niwas Chowk houses 4 gates where each has a different role to play and a story to tell. These gates represent four seasons and Hindu gods and goddesses. The Peacock Gate with motifs of peacocks represents autumn and is dedicated to Lord Vishnu. The Lotus Gate has continual flower and petal pattern representing summer season and is dedicated to Lord Shiva and Parvati. The Green Gate is suggestive of spring and is dedicated to Lord Ganesha. Lastly, the Rose Gate with repeated petal pattern represents winter season and is dedicated to Goddess Devi.

Mubarak Mahal (the palace of reception)- Mainly a reception centre, this Mahal is now being converted into a museum showcasing a wide exclusive collection of Rajasthani textiles worn by kings and queens of the state like delicate Pashmina (Kashmiri) shawls, royal costumes, Benaras silk sarees, and other dresses with Sanganeri prints & folk embroidery.

Diwan-e-Khas (Sarvato Bhadra)- Also known as the 'Hall of Private Audience'. Decorated with plush chandeliers and once a private audience hall of the royals it is now an art gallery. Interestingly, the hall also features two huge sterling silver vessels which are officially recorded as the world’s largest sterling silver vessels.

Diwan-e-Aam (Sabha Niwas)- Also known as the 'Hall of Public Audience', it has it's ceiling painted in rich red and gold colors. It is a museum with wide variety of display of art gallery, miniature paintings, ancient texts, embroidered rugs, Kashmir shawls and carpets.

Chandra Mahal- Chandra Mahal is a seven-storeyed building and each floor has been named like the Sukh Niwas (the house of pleasure), Rang Mahal or Shobha Niwas, Chhavi Niwas, Shri Niwas, and Mukut Mandir. It contains of paintings, mirror work and floral decorations. There is beautiful peacock gate at the entry of the Mahal.The flag at the top of Chandra Mahal belongs to the royal family and is only unfurled when the Maharaja is in the palace.

Govind Dev Ji Temple- At this temple of Lord Krishna, built in 18th century you can see the European chandeliers and Indian paintings. The temple has a gold ceiling. The aarti (prayer offering) is offered seven times in a day.

Maharani’s Palace- Served as the residence of the queens, this royal palace is adorned with gems and semi-precious stones. It is now a museum displaying weapons. The intricate ceiling of this mahal is comprising of beautiful precious stones.

Baggi Khana- It is a museum dedicated for displaying collection of old chariots and coaches.
